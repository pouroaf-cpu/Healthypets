If your cat is scratching, biting at the base of its tail, or you've spotted tiny dark specks in its fur, you're almost certainly dealing with fleas — and you want them gone. The short answer: for most New Zealand cats, a **monthly all-in-one spot-on like Revolution Plus** is the easiest, most reliable fix, because it handles fleas *and* common worms in a single dose. If money's tight, **Advantage** kills fleas fast and costs less. Whichever you choose, the part everyone forgets is the bit that actually ends the cycle — treating your *house*.

Here's how to do it properly, what to buy in NZ, and how to make sure they don't come straight back.

## First, why fleas are so hard to shift

The flea you'll find on a Kiwi cat is almost always the cat flea, *Ctenocephalides felis* — and it's the same flea that bites dogs and, occasionally, you. The reason a single cat can trigger a household infestation is simple maths: the adult fleas living on your cat are only about **5% of the total population**. The other **95% — eggs, larvae and pupae — are in your carpet, the couch, and the cat's bed**, waiting to hatch.

<Callout type="nz" title="It's not just a summer thing in NZ">
Flea season peaks over a New Zealand summer, but in the warmer top half of the North Island — and in any heated home — fleas breed happily year-round. Indoors, a mild Kiwi winter doesn't kill them off. That's why "I'll stop treating over winter" is how most re-infestations start.
</Callout>

That biology is the whole reason this guide exists: treat only the cat and you'll be back here in three weeks. Treat the cat **and** the environment, on schedule, and you win.

## How to actually get rid of fleas (the 3-step protocol)

1. **Treat the cat with a vet-quality product.** Pick one from the table below and apply it correctly — spot-ons go on the skin at the back of the neck where your cat can't lick it off, not on the fur.
2. **Treat your home.** Vacuum floors, rugs and skirting (then bin the vacuum bag or empty it outside), hot-wash all pet bedding, and use a household flea spray on carpets and soft furnishings. The vibration and warmth of vacuuming even helps coax pupae out of hiding so the treatment reaches them.
3. **Treat every animal in the house, and keep going.** One untreated cat or dog re-seeds the whole problem. Then stay on a monthly schedule for at least three months to break the full life cycle.

<Callout type="safety" title="Never put a dog flea product on a cat">
Some dog flea treatments contain **permethrin**, which is **highly toxic — even fatal — to cats**. Always use a cat-specific product, and if you have both pets, keep a newly-treated dog away from your cat until the spot is dry. When in doubt, check with your vet.
</Callout>

## Spot-on, tablet or collar — which type?

- **Spot-ons (Revolution Plus, Advantage, Advocate)** — a liquid you part the fur and apply to the skin. Easiest for cats that hate tablets, which is most of them. The default choice in NZ.
- **Long-acting options (Bravecto)** — same idea, fewer doses. Great if you travel or just forget.
- **Collars (Seresto)** — steady protection for months with nothing to remember, as long as your cat tolerates a collar and you use a quick-release safety collar.

## Will the cheap supermarket stuff do?

Honestly, usually not. Budget sprays, powders and shampoos from the supermarket tend to be older chemistry that fleas in NZ have partly shrugged off, and they don't stop the eggs and larvae in your home. You can burn more money topping them up than you'd have spent on one good spot-on. The one place a cheap product earns its keep is the **home** side — a household flea spray plus a hot wash of the bedding is money well spent alongside a proper on-cat treatment.

<Callout type="vet" title="When to call the vet">
See your vet if your cat has scabby, raw skin (it may have a flea-allergy, which needs more than flea control), if kittens are very young or unwell, or if a heavy infestation isn't clearing after a month of doing everything right. Flea-borne tapeworm is also common after an infestation — ask about worming at the same time.
</Callout>
